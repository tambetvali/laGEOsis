### Spherical Octahedron Naming
#### Why its triangles and points have no traditional individual names

The short answer: a spherical octahedron does not have special traditional names for its individual spherical triangles or its extreme points. The vertices and faces are normally just referred to using the standard octahedral symmetry labels, not unique names.

Below is the full breakdown so you can see what is named, what isn’t, and how mathematicians typically refer to these features.

#### 🧭 What is named in an octahedron’s spherical version
A spherical octahedron is simply the projection of a regular octahedron onto the unit sphere. Its structure is governed by octahedral symmetry (the group 𝑂ₕ), which is well‑documented in spherical geometry and symmetry-group literature.

Under this symmetry:

- The 6 vertices correspond to the coordinate axes: ±X, ±Y, ±Z  
  These are sometimes called the poles or axis points, but not given individual names.

- The 8 spherical triangles are the spherical images of the octahedron’s faces.  
  They are all congruent and are simply called faces or fundamental domains of the octahedral group.

- The 12 edges are great‑circle arcs lying in the planes defined by pairs of coordinate axes.

None of these receive special traditional names beyond these symmetry-based descriptors.
